What Is the 7/7 Testing Standard for Research Peptides?

📅 January 20, 2025 ✍️ MyPeptidePrice Research Team ⏱ 6 min read 🔄 Updated 2025
Quick Answer

The 7/7 testing standard requires research peptide vendors to provide independent lab results across 7 categories: net purity, net content, heavy metals, endotoxins, identification, sterility, and conformity. Vendors who only publish purity results (the most common shortcut) are passing 1 of 7 — not the full standard.

What Is the 7/7 Testing Standard?

The 7/7 testing standard is a quality benchmark used to evaluate research peptide vendors. A vendor meets the standard only when independent third-party labs confirm their products pass all seven of the following tests — with no test skipped and no in-house results accepted.

The seven tests are:

  1. Net Purity — Confirms the percentage of active compound. A vial labeled 99% pure should contain at least 99% of the target peptide, with the remainder being harmless excipients, not degradation products.
  2. Net Content — Confirms the actual milligram amount in the vial matches what is advertised. A 5mg vial should contain 5mg — not 3.8mg.
  3. Heavy Metals — Screens for toxic trace metals including lead, arsenic, mercury, and cadmium that can appear as manufacturing contaminants.
  4. Endotoxins — Tests for bacterial endotoxins (pyrogens) that are invisible to purity tests but can cause serious biological reactions at even very low concentrations.
  5. Identification — Confirms the compound is actually the peptide it claims to be. Without this test, a vial could contain a cheaper or unrelated compound with similar characteristics.
  6. Sterility — Confirms the product is free from viable microbial contamination including bacteria, yeast, and mold.
  7. Conformity — Confirms the product's lot number, specifications, and test results match the published Certificate of Analysis.

Why Do Most Peptide Vendors Only Run 1 or 2 Tests?

HPLC purity testing — the most commonly cited test — costs relatively little and produces a clean, marketable result. Running all seven tests adds significant cost and time per batch, which cuts into margins for vendors operating at scale.

The result: most vendors publish a single purity number and call it a "full CoA." That is not a full CoA. A purity result tells you the compound is reasonably clean — but it cannot detect heavy metals, endotoxins, or whether your vial actually contains 5mg instead of 3mg.

As of 2025, MyPeptidePrice.com only lists vendors who publish complete 7/7 CoAs from named independent labs for every product in their catalog.

How Do You Read a Peptide Certificate of Analysis?

When reviewing any vendor's CoA, check these three things before trusting the result:

  • Who ran the test? The lab name should be a recognized third-party testing facility — not "in-house" or the vendor's own name. Look for lab names like Janssen Analytical, Colmaric Analyticals, or similar accredited facilities.
  • How old is it? A CoA dated 18 months ago does not reflect your current batch. Reputable vendors update CoAs with each new production run. Results older than 12 months for the current batch are a flag.
  • Are all 7 categories covered? Scan the document. If you see only "Purity: 99.1%" with no mention of heavy metals, endotoxins, or sterility, the CoA is incomplete regardless of how it's labeled.

What Is the Difference Between Purity and Identity Testing?

Purity testing (HPLC) tells you how much of the sample is the target compound. Identity testing (MS, NMR) tells you which compound it is. Both are required for a complete quality picture.

A high purity result on the wrong compound still means you received the wrong product. Identity testing is the safeguard that confirms the correct peptide sequence before purity even matters.

Does Price Per mg Change When You Factor in Testing Quality?

Yes — significantly. A vial that is 65% pure and priced at $30 costs more per effective milligram than a fully tested 99% pure vial at $45. Real cost per mg = (price) ÷ (mg × purity%). Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 7/7 testing standard for research peptides?

The 7/7 testing standard requires a vendor to provide independent lab results for seven tests: net purity, net content, heavy metals, endotoxins, identification, sterility, and conformity. All seven must be performed by a third-party lab with no vendor affiliation.

Why do most peptide vendors only test for purity?

HPLC purity testing is the cheapest and fastest test available. Running all seven tests costs significantly more per batch and takes longer, so vendors who prioritize margins over transparency typically skip the remaining six tests.

How do I read a peptide Certificate of Analysis?

Check: (1) the lab name — it should be a named third party, not in-house; (2) the date — within 12 months of your batch; (3) coverage — all 7 test categories should be listed. A CoA showing only purity is incomplete.

Which peptide vendors meet the full 7/7 standard?

As of 2025, vendors tracked by MyPeptidePrice.com that meet the 7/7 standard include Southern Aminos, Ion Peptide, Glacier Aminos, LabSourced Peptides, Mile High Peptides, and Instant Peptides. All are verified before listing.

Does passing 7/7 testing guarantee a peptide is safe?

The 7/7 standard confirms product quality and purity within the context of research-grade material. These products are sold for laboratory research purposes only and are not intended for human use or consumption.
